\section{Development of tire production in the U.S. }

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:figure1}, U.S. domestic tire production was largely stagnant at the beginning of the 21st century. Production began at a level of 127.5 and ended at 124.3 (the year 2017 serves as the base year) by the third quarter of 2007. Between 2008 and 2010, production experienced a sharp decline up to 20 percentage points. Although production initially saw a dramatic recovery in 2010, it quickly returned to a state of stagnation. Subsequently, the COVID-19 Pandemic triggered another severe contraction, with production dropping by more than 20 percent, though it rebounded by roughly 20 percent shortly after. Following this recovery, production began to stagnate and eventually after Russian-Ukrainian war started contracting even more. By the fourth quarter of 2020, the production level stood at 99.8, representing a slight decrease from Q4 2019, and ultimately fell to 72.8 by Q1 2026. 

The median growth rate stands at 0.43 percent and a mean at -0.58 percent. The maximum and minimum growth values were recorded at 22.04 percent in Q2 2021 and -22.82 percent in Q2 2020, respectively. The standard deviation of growth rates is 5.89 and for volumes of production stands at nearly 7.48. The median growth of the total US industrial production is nearly 1.63 and the mean is 0.58 percentage points. In comparison to the tire industry growth both values are higher. Although the maximum rate of growth was lower at 16.55 percent in 2021 in comparison to the tire industry, but the minimum value is larger as it stands at -17.31 in 2020. Both largest recoveries and slumps occurred in the same period during COVID-19 Pandemic.

Figure \ref{fig:figure3} shows a right‑skewed histogram of tire imports to the US in 2025 from the forty countries with the largest import volumes. The graph indicates that the most countries supply from few hundreds to few thousands of tires while a small number supply very large quantities. \section{Comparison part}
The 4 compared values are Producer price index, Import price index, Consumer price index, and harmonized price index, in the tire industry. The producer price index measures the change in expanses for domesticate producers in the specific industry, and the import price index measures the change in the price of imported goods. Harmonized index is created using the total volume of tires imported to the USA, divided by the total value of imported tires. The consumer price index measures the price of the good for consumers.
We can see relatively strong correlation between all four values. As all of them are connected to the price of tires, it can be assumed, that certain factors, such as the price of raw materials, the demand for tires or global economic crises, would affect these values in a similar way. Therefore, it is not surprising that all correlations of these values are very high and between 0.87 and 0.98. We can also look at their average growth using CAGR coefficient, it can be argued that in this situation it’s superior to average change, as it is more informative about total difference. Greatest change of about 0.25\% per month was in PPI, HS was about 0.21\% and IPI and CPI were both under 0.16\% per month. This implies that domestic prices grew faster than imported, and it can be assumed that this could have created some decline in domestic production. All values sharply increased in early 2020s, and while PPI remained high, all other values slightly decreased.
